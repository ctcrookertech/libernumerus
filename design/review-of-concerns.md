## Concern 1: Ogun associated with 7 [A:264]

**Citation review's concern:** No specific textual source cited for the Ogun–7 association.

**Assessment: The association is defensible but should be clarified in its sourcing.**

The link between Ogun and the number 7 operates through two channels. In Ifá divination, it derives from Ogun's connection to the Odù Ogunda, which occupies the 7th position in many (not all) Yoruba lineage rankings of the 16 principal Odù. William Bascom documents the ranking systems and their variation across lineages in *Ifa Divination: Communication Between Gods and Men in West Africa* (Indiana University Press, 1969), pp. 3–20 and the appendices on Odù ordering. Wande Abimbola's *Ifá: An Exposition of Ifá Literary Corpus* (Oxford University Press, 1976) elaborates on the mythological narratives linking Orishas to specific Odù. In diasporic Lukumí/Santería practice, where Orisha-number associations became more rigidly systematized, 7 for Ogun is canonical — documented in Joseph Murphy's *Santería: African Spirits in America* (Beacon, 1993) and Migene González-Wippler's *Santería: The Religion* (Harmony, 1989).

The complication is that Odù ranking varies by lineage, and the specific position of Ogunda is not universally fixed at 7. The document's statement is accurate for the most widely cited ranking, but the variability should be acknowledged.

**Suggested fix for [A:264]:** No change strictly necessary, but adding a parenthetical like "in many Ifá lineage rankings" after "Associated with Ogun" would add precision.

## Concern 2: Shango associated with 6 and 12 [A:267]

**Citation review's concern:** No specific source for this number-deity association.

**Assessment: Correct and well-sourced in the tradition, though the provenance is cleaner in diasporic than in continental Yoruba practice.**

Shango's association with 6 traces through his connection to the Odù Obara (6th in many rankings) and through the diasporic Lukumí tradition where the number 6 is his canonical ritual number — the number of his otanes (sacred stones), offerings, and rhythmic patterns. Pierre Fatumbi Verger documents the Obara-Shango linkage in *Orixás: Deuses Iorubás na África e no Novo Mundo* (Corrupio, 1981). Lydia Cabrera's *El Monte* (Ediciones Universal, 1954/1975) and Robert Farris Thompson's *Flash of the Spirit* (Random House, 1983) both document the 6-Shango association in Cuban practice. 12 follows naturally as 6 doubled.

In the Ifá corpus proper, the association is narrative and contextual rather than a fixed table of correspondences — which is worth noting, since the document's brief format risks implying a canonical number-assignment that is more rigid than the oral tradition warrants.

**Suggested fix for [A:267]:** Consider rewording to: "Shango associated with 6 and 12 in many Ifá lineage traditions and in diasporic practice — thunder, justice, royal power."

## Concern 3: Oya associated with 9 [A:266]

**Citation review's concern:** No specific source cited.

**Assessment: This is one of the most secure Orisha-number associations and needs no correction, only a note on sourcing.**

Oya's link to 9 is among the most consistent across both Yoruba and diasporic traditions. In Ifá, her association with the Odù Osa (which occupies the 9th position in the most widely cited rankings) is documented in Abimbola (1976) and Bascom (1969). Mythological narratives describe Oya in sets of nine — nine children, nine transformations, nine as the boundary between the living and the dead. In Lukumí practice, 9 is her unambiguous ritual number. Verger (1981) and Thompson (1983) both document this. The association also persists in Brazilian Candomblé (see Juana Elbein dos Santos, *Os Nàgô e a Morte*, Vozes, 1976).

**Suggested fix:** None needed. This is solid.

## Concern 4: 11 incense spices of the Temple [A:73]

**Citation review's concern:** No Talmudic citation given in the document.

**Assessment: Accurate claim; the source is straightforward and well-known.**

The 11 spices of the Temple incense (Pittum HaKetoret) are enumerated in the Baraita of the Incense, which appears in Talmud Bavli, Keritot 6a. The 11 ingredients are: balsam, onycha, galbanum, frankincense, myrrh, cassia, spikenard, saffron, costus, aromatic bark, and cinnamon — with additional components (Carshina lye, Cyprus wine, Sodom salt, a smoke-raising herb) that serve as processing agents rather than counted among the core 11. The association of the number 11 with Kaf (כ), the 11th Hebrew letter, is a standard Kabbalistic correspondence found in many commentaries on the Sefer Yetzirah and in later Hasidic sources.

**Suggested fix:** None needed in the document itself. The citation file could note "Talmud Bavli, Keritot 6a" as the source.

## Concern 5: 72 forms of Ra [B:175] [C:259]

**Citation review's concern:** "Some late-period texts" is vague. Which texts?

**Assessment: This claim is problematic and should be corrected or significantly qualified.**

This is the weakest factual claim the citation audit flagged, and the reviewer was right to press on it. The commonly cited "72 forms of Ra" does not correspond cleanly to any single Egyptian primary text. The *Litany of Ra* (Litanei des Re), the most relevant source — found in New Kingdom royal tombs beginning with Thutmose III (KV34) and reaching its fullest form in the tomb of Seti I (KV17) — catalogs 75 invocations or forms of Ra, not 72. The standard scholarly edition is Erik Hornung's *Das Buch der Anbetung des Re im Westen* (Ägyptologische Abhandlungen 2–3, 1975–76; English: *The Egyptian Amduat*, though the Litany is a separate text treated in Hornung's *Valley of the Kings*, 1990). Alexandre Piankoff's earlier translation (*The Litany of Re*, Bollingen Series XL.4, 1964) also counts 75.

The number 72 for this context appears to be a modern numerological harmonization — possibly influenced by the fact that Plutarch's *De Iside* (ch. 13) gives 72 as the number of Set's conspirators, and that 72 has independent Kabbalistic significance (the Shem HaMephorash). The "72 forms of Ra" formulation circulates widely in popular esoteric literature but I cannot trace it to a specific Egyptian-language primary source with that count.

**Suggested fix for [B:175]:** Change "the 72 forms of Ra" to "the numerous forms of Ra catalogued in the Litany of Ra (75 invocations in the standard version)."

**Suggested fix for [C:259]:** Rewrite the Egyptian entry under 72 to read: "**Egyptian:** Set enlisted 72 conspirators to murder Osiris (Plutarch, *De Iside et Osiride*, ch. 13 — a Greek source reporting Egyptian myth). The *Litany of Ra* in New Kingdom royal tombs catalogs 75 invocations of Ra in his various forms; the widespread citation of '72 forms of Ra' in popular literature appears to be a modern approximation or harmonization rather than an attested Egyptian count."

## Concern 6: 9 mothers of Heimdall [A:248] [B:229] [C:273]

**Citation review's concern:** Attested in *Hyndluljóð* 35–40 but the documents don't cite the specific source.

**Assessment: Accurate. The source situation is known and can be specified.**

The nine mothers of Heimdall are named in *Hyndluljóð* (Song of Hyndla) stanzas 35–37 (numbering varies by edition). This poem survives in the *Flateyjarbók* (GKS 1005 fol., c. 1387–94) rather than in the Codex Regius. The nine mothers are listed as: Gjálp, Greip, Eistla, Eyrgjafa, Úlfrún, Angeyja, Imðr, Atla, and Járnsaxa. Snorri references Heimdall's multiple mothers in *Gylfaginning* 27 without specifying the number, and *Völuspá hin skamma* (preserved within *Hyndluljóð* as stanza 38 in some editions) confirms the tradition. John Lindow's *Norse Mythology: A Guide to the Gods, Heroes, Rituals, and Beliefs* (Oxford, 2001) and Rudolf Simek's *Dictionary of Northern Mythology* (D.S. Brewer, 1993; tr. Angela Hall) both discuss the nine mothers and their sources. The tradition is secure.

**Suggested fix:** None needed in the documents. The citation file should note *Hyndluljóð* 35–37 (Flateyjarbók) as the primary source.

## Concern 8: Hitobashira [A:154]

**Citation review's concern:** The term hitobashira refers to human sacrifice, not a "sacred pillar" as axis of a shrine.

**Assessment: This is a genuine error in the document and should be corrected.**

The citation review is correct. 人柱 (*hitobashira*) literally means "human pillar" and refers to the practice — attested in Japanese folklore and some historical accounts — of burying a living person in the foundation of a structure (castle, bridge, dam, seawall) as a sacrificial offering to ensure its stability. The practice is documented in numerous *setsuwa* (tale literature) collections and local legends; the scholarly treatment is in Amino Yoshihiko's work on medieval Japanese society and in Yanagita Kunio's folklore studies. The most famous literary instance is the legend of Matsue Castle (Matsue-jō hitobashira).

The architectural feature the document likely intended to reference is the 心御柱 (*shin no mihashira*), the sacred central pillar of Shinto shrine architecture. At the Ise Grand Shrine (Ise Jingū), the *shin no mihashira* is the hidden central post around which the shrine is constructed and which is ritually renewed every 20 years during the Shikinen Sengū. This pillar functions as the axis mundi of the shrine — the "singular" vertical axis connecting heaven and earth. It is discussed in scholarship on Ise, including Mark Teeuwen and John Breen, *A Social History of the Ise Shrines* (Bloomsbury, 2017), and in Aston's and Philippi's discussions of Shinto architecture.

The two concepts are not only different but carry opposite connotations — one is a ritual sacrifice, the other a sacred architectural element. This is not a minor semantic issue.

**Suggested fix for [A:154]:** Replace the current text with:

`| 1 | **Hitotsu.** Beginning, the singular, the first. In Shinto, the shin no mihashira (心御柱, sacred central pillar) as axis of the shrine. |`

## Concern 9: 5 aicme of Ogham [A:322] vs. 4 aicme [B:281]

**Citation review's concern:** Internal inconsistency between the two documents.

**Assessment: The methodology file [B:281] is correct. The systems file [A:322] is nonstandard and misleading.**

The standard Ogham alphabet consists of 4 *aicme* (groups) of 5 letters each, totaling 20 letters. These are sometimes called the aicme of B, H, M, and A (after their first letters). A fifth group, the *forfeda* ("additional letters"), was added later and appears in the Ogham Tract in the Book of Ballymote and other medieval manuscripts. The standard scholarly reference is Damian McManus, *A Guide to Ogam* (An Sagart, Maynooth Monographs in Celtic Studies 4, 1991). McManus treats the forfeda as supplementary, not as a fifth aicme on equal standing with the original four.

The systems file [A:322] ties the number 5 to "the 5 aicme (groups) of Ogham" as part of its case for the significance of 5 in Celtic symbolism. This stretches the evidence — counting the forfeda as a co-equal fifth aicme is not how the tradition was understood historically. The 5 provinces of Ireland are a much stronger association for the number 5 in Celtic symbolism and can carry the point alone.

**Suggested fix for [A:322]:** Rewrite to: 

`| 5 | The 5 provinces of Ireland, the 5 original vowels of Ogham. Centrality, sovereignty. |`

This preserves a genuine Ogham connection to 5 (the five vowels — *aicme* of A: Ailm, Onn, Úr, Eadhadh, Iodhadh — form a distinct structural group) without the nonstandard "5 aicme" claim.

Alternatively, if the author wants to keep the *aicme* reference: "The 5 provinces of Ireland. The Ogham alphabet's 4 aicme of 5 letters each (with a later supplementary group of forfeda) are structured around the number 5."

## Concern 10: Verdi and 432 Hz [C:698]

**Citation review's concern:** The claim is "based on his 1884 letter" and the details require verification.

**Assessment: The document conflates two distinct historical facts and should be corrected.**

The relevant primary source is a letter Verdi wrote in 1884 to the Italian Music Commission, which has been widely discussed in the literature on historical pitch. The key scholarly reference is Bruce Haynes, *A History of Performing Pitch: The Story of "A"* (Scarecrow Press, 2002), which traces pitch standards from the Renaissance to the modern era.

The historical facts are:

The French *diapason normal*, established by law in 1859, set A = 435 Hz (at 15°C). It was not 432. This standard was based on a commission that included Berlioz and others, and was an administrative attempt to arrest the upward creep of orchestral pitch.

Verdi did advocate for a lower Italian standard. In his correspondence with the Music Commission, he expressed a preference for A = 432 Hz — a figure slightly below the French standard. His reasoning, as best we can reconstruct it from the letters, was primarily about vocal comfort (particularly for singers performing in the Italian operatic tradition, where upper tessituras are taxing). An Italian governmental decree in 1885 did briefly establish A = 432 Hz as a standard, partly on Verdi's influence, though enforcement was minimal and the standard was widely ignored.

The document at [C:698] states: "his preferred standard (A=432 Hz at the French diapason normal)." This is the conflation — 432 was Verdi's preferred number, but it was *not* the French diapason normal (which was 435). The parenthetical implies 432 and the French standard were the same thing.

**Suggested fix for [C:698]:** Change the final sentence to: "Giuseppe Verdi did advocate for a lower pitch standard in the 19th century. His letters to the Italian Music Commission (1884) expressed a preference for A=432 Hz — slightly below the French *diapason normal* of A=435 Hz (established 1859) — based on vocal ergonomics and singer comfort, not mystical numerology."

## Cross-Reference Issue 1: Tarot 8/11 swap [A:349]

**Citation review's concern:** The grouping of "Marseille/Golden Dawn" against "Rider-Waite" may be inaccurate given the Golden Dawn's complex internal symbolism.

**Assessment: The document's statement is correct at the level of card numbering and needs no change.**

The Tarot de Marseille tradition numbers Justice as VIII and La Force (Strength) as XI. The Golden Dawn adopted this same external numbering in its published materials (as documented in Israel Regardie's *The Golden Dawn*, 1937–40; later expanded editions). Waite, in designing the Rider-Waite-Smith deck (1909), swapped the numbers: Strength became VIII and Justice became XI. His motivation appears to have been alignment with astrological sequence (placing Leo/Strength before Libra/Justice in zodiacal order along the paths of the Tree of Life).

The citation review correctly notes that the Golden Dawn's internal path attributions are more complex than the card numbers alone suggest — for instance, the Hebrew letter and astrological assignments to specific paths don't change just because a card number changes, and the Golden Dawn's members were working with letter-path correspondences more than card numbers per se. But the document is talking about card numbering specifically, and on that point, grouping the Marseille and Golden Dawn together against Rider-Waite is accurate.

**Suggested fix:** None needed. The statement is correct for what it claims.

## Cross-Reference Issue 2: Nesimi death date [B:109]

**Citation review's concern:** "d. ~1417" — scholarly estimates range from 1404 to 1418.

**Assessment: The tilde is appropriate; no change needed.**

The primary sources for Nesimi's execution are Ottoman and Timurid chronicles that give varying dates. Shahab Shahbazi's work on the Hurufis, Bashir's *Fazlallah Astarabadi and the Hurufis* (Oneworld, 2005), and Mir-Kasimov's *Words of Power* (I.B. Tauris, 2015) all discuss the dating uncertainty. The most commonly cited dates in recent scholarship are 1417 or 1418 (the earlier date of 1404, based on certain Ottoman sources, has fallen out of favor but is not definitively ruled out). The tilde adequately signals the uncertainty.

**Suggested fix:** None needed.
