# Numerology Systems

*Meanings of numbers 1–13 across 17 traditions*

---

## Pythagorean / Western

**Era:** 6th c. BCE (Pythagorean); modern popular numerology from 19th–20th c.

The ancient Pythagorean tradition was concerned with the Decad (numbers 1–10). Numbers 11–13 and the concept of "Master Numbers" are products of 20th-century popular numerology and have no connection to Pythagoras or ancient Pythagorean mathematical philosophy.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **The Monad.** Unity, origin, the source of all number. Independence, individuality, leadership, creation. |
| 2 | **The Dyad.** Duality, polarity, receptivity, partnership, balance, division. |
| 3 | **The Triad.** Synthesis, creativity, expression, joy, the first 'true' number (beginning-middle-end). |
| 4 | **The Tetrad.** Stability, order, foundation, hard work, the 4 elements, materiality. |
| 5 | **The Pentad.** Change, freedom, adventure, the senses, dynamism. The marriage of 2+3. |
| 6 | **The Hexad.** Harmony, responsibility, love, domesticity, beauty. The first 'perfect number' (1+2+3). |
| 7 | **The Heptad.** Introspection, wisdom, mystery, spirituality, analysis. Sacred and set apart. |
| 8 | **The Ogdoad.** Power, material mastery, abundance, cosmic balance. |
| 9 | **The Ennead.** Completion, humanitarianism, universal love, endings. Contains all prior numbers. |
| 10 | **The Decad.** Return to unity (1+0=1). Perfection, completion of the cycle. The Tetractys (1+2+3+4). |
| 11 | *(Modern addition)* Master Number. Spiritual insight, intuition, illumination, visionary idealism. Not reduced. |
| 12 | *(Modern reduction method)* Reduced to 3. Cosmic order (12 months, 12 signs). Creative completion. |
| 13 | *(Modern reduction method)* Reduced to 4. Transformation, upheaval. Sometimes feared, sometimes a sign of powerful change. |

## Chaldean (Babylonian)

**Era:** Modern system (popularized late 19th c. by Cheiro and others) claiming ancient Babylonian roots. Authentic Babylonian number symbolism (based on sexagesimal mathematics, astronomical cycles, and ritual texts) is a distinct tradition from which this system borrows its name but little of its content.

The planetary attributions of Uranus and Neptune are anachronistic — these planets were discovered in 1781 and 1846, respectively. Rahu and Ketu are Vedic (Indian) lunar node concepts with no basis in Mesopotamian cosmology. The letter-to-number mapping used in this system is a modern reconstruction, not attested in cuneiform sources. The rule that 9 is "not assigned to any letter" originates with Cheiro (Count Louis Hamon), not with ancient Babylonian practice.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Sun.** Individuality, ambition, action, the primal creative force. |
| 2 | **Moon.** Imagination, sensitivity, duality, fluctuation, the feminine principle. |
| 3 | **Jupiter.** Expansion, optimism, expression, talent, ambition. |
| 4 | **Rahu (attributed to Uranus in some modern versions).** Rebellion, unconventionality, sudden change, hidden opposition. |
| 5 | **Mercury.** Versatility, communication, restlessness, commerce, wit. |
| 6 | **Venus.** Love, beauty, magnetism, harmony, artistic sensibility. |
| 7 | **Ketu (attributed to Neptune in some modern versions).** Mystery, spirituality, psychic ability, otherworldliness, the occult. |
| 8 | **Saturn.** Fate, hardship, delayed success, karmic lessons, material struggle then mastery. |
| 9 | **Mars.** Sacred number — not assigned to any letter in the modern Chaldean system. Conflict, energy, completion, the divine. |
| 10 | **Wheel of Fortune.** Cyclical rise and fall, honor or dishonor, renewal. |
| 11 | Hidden dangers, treachery, trials. Also spiritual testing and karmic debt. |
| 12 | **The Sacrifice / Victim.** Self-undoing, anxiety, but also spiritual surrender. |
| 13 | Transformation, power through upheaval. Associated with death-and-rebirth. Potent, not simply unlucky. |

## Hebrew Gematria (Kabbalah)

**Era:** Gematria practice from ~2nd c. BCE onward; the Sefirotic associations described below reflect the mature Kabbalistic framework (12th–13th c. CE, Zohar era)

In Kabbalistic tradition, the Hebrew letters are associated with the 22 *paths* connecting the Sefirot on the Tree of Life, not with the Sefirot themselves — e.g. Aleph is the path between Keter and Chokmah, not a synonym for Keter. The Sefirot listed below correspond to the *numerical position* (1=Keter, 2=Chokmah, etc.), not to the letter directly.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Aleph (א).** The Infinite (Ein Sof), unity, the breath of God. Keter (Crown) is the first Sefirah. The unmanifest. |
| 2 | **Bet (ב).** Duality, creation (Bereshit begins with Bet), the house. Chokmah (Wisdom) is the second Sefirah. |
| 3 | **Gimel (ג).** Kindness, reward, movement. Binah (Understanding) is the third Sefirah. The camel — carrying across distances. |
| 4 | **Dalet (ד).** Door, poverty, humility, receptivity. The physical world, the 4 matriarchs. |
| 5 | **He (ה).** Breath, spirit, divine grace. The 5 books of Torah. Window to the divine. |
| 6 | **Vav (ו).** Connection, hook, linking heaven and earth. The 6 directions of physical space. Tiferet (Beauty). |
| 7 | **Zayin (ז).** Shabbat, rest, completion of creation, spiritual sustenance, the sword of discernment. |
| 8 | **Chet (ח).** Life (חי, chai = 18 = 8 in reduced form), transcendence beyond nature, covenant, new beginning. |
| 9 | **Tet (ט).** Goodness hidden within, pregnancy, the concealed, truth gestating before revelation. |
| 10 | **Yod (י).** The hand of God, the primordial point, divine spark. Smallest letter containing all others. |
| 11 | **Kaf (כ).** The palm, crowning, containment. Also associated with the 11 incense spices of the Temple. |
| 12 | **Lamed (ל).** Learning, teaching, the ox-goad. 12 tribes of Israel. Aspiration — tallest letter. |
| 13 | **Mem (מ).** Water, the womb, hidden wisdom. Gematria of אחד (Echad, 'One') = 13 = אהבה (Ahavah, 'Love'). Deeply sacred. |

## Greek Isopsephy

**Era:** ~5th c. BCE onward

In Greek isopsephy, numerical values follow the alphabet including archaic letters: Alpha=1, Beta=2, Gamma=3, Delta=4, Epsilon=5, Digamma/Stigma=6, Zeta=7, Eta=8, Theta=9, Iota=10.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Alpha.** The beginning, the Monad, primordial unity. Zeus as first principle. |
| 2 | **Beta.** Duality, differentiation, the Dyad. Feminine principle in Pythagorean thought. |
| 3 | **Gamma.** The Triad. Completeness (beginning-middle-end). Associated with Hecate and the triple goddess. |
| 4 | **Delta.** The Tetrad. Earth, solidity, justice (Pythagorean). The 4 elements. |
| 5 | **Epsilon.** Marriage (2+3, female+male). Health, the Pentagram. Hieros gamos. |
| 6 | **Digamma (Ϝ) / Stigma (ϛ).** The archaic letter with value 6. Perfection, harmony (1×2×3). Aphrodite in some traditions. |
| 7 | **Zeta.** Athena / the virgin. Kairos (opportune moment). Holiness, the 7 vowels in Greek magic. |
| 8 | **Eta.** Justice, cosmic equilibrium. In later Gnostic thought (Valentinian, 2nd c. CE), the Ogdoad — the 8th sphere beyond the 7 planets. |
| 9 | **Theta.** Completion, the horizon. Also associated with death (θάνατος). |
| 10 | **Iota.** The Decad / Tetractys. Perfection, all-encompassing unity. Sacred to the Pythagoreans. |
| 12 | **The Dodecad.** 12 Olympian gods, 12 zodiac signs, cosmic governance. |
| 13 | Considered unlucky in some Greek contexts. Associated with the 13th at a table (myth of discord). |

## Arabic Abjad / Ilm al-Huruf

**Era:** ~7th c. CE onward (Abjad numerals); Ilm al-Huruf ("Science of Letters") as a developed esoteric tradition from ~9th–10th c. CE onward

The broader tradition of Islamic letter-number mysticism includes the Hurufi movement (founded by Fazlallah Astarabadi, 14th c.), which developed elaborate correspondences between letters, numbers, the human face and body, prophetic cycles, and eschatological time. The entries below represent the mainstream Abjad and Sufi frameworks; Hurufism constitutes a more radical extension in which the entire cosmos is legible as divine script, and the 28 (or 32) letters of the Arabic and Persian alphabets encode the structure of creation itself.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Alif (ا).** The One (Al-Ahad), divine unity (Tawhid), the vertical axis, the Pen (al-Qalam). |
| 2 | **Ba (ب).** Duality, the first creation, the point beneath Ba as the origin of existence (Sufi teaching, especially Ibn Arabi). |
| 3 | **Jim (ج).** Beauty, totality (beginning-middle-end), the 3 worlds (mulk, malakut, jabarut). |
| 4 | **Dal (د).** The 4 elements, the 4 archangels, materiality, the square, stability. |
| 5 | **Ha (ه).** The 5 pillars of Islam, the 5 daily prayers, the hand of Fatima (Khamsa). |
| 6 | **Waw (و).** The 6 days of creation, connection, the hook linking realms. |
| 7 | **Za (ز).** The 7 heavens, 7 earths, 7 verses of al-Fatiha, 7 circuits of tawaf. Cosmic completeness. |
| 8 | **Ha' (ح).** The 8 angels bearing the Throne. Paradise has 8 gates. Abundance. |
| 9 | **Ta (ط).** Gestation, the 9 months, hiddenness, the completion before return. |
| 10 | **Ya (ي).** Return to origin, the divine hand, cyclical renewal. |
| 12 | The 12 months, the 12 Imams (in Shia Islam), cosmic administration. |
| 13 | Significant in Shia esotericism (the 12 Imams + the Hidden Imam / Mahdi). Transformation. |

## Chinese Cosmological

**Era:** ~3000 BCE onward

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Yī (一).** Yang, origin, heaven, the Dao as undivided unity. Auspicious. |
| 2 | **Èr (二).** Yin-Yang polarity, pairing, balance. 'Good things come in pairs.' The earth. |
| 3 | **Sān (三).** Birth — 'Dao begets one, one begets two, two begets three, three begets the myriad things.' Growth, creativity. |

| 6 | **Liù (六).** Smooth, flowing, 'everything going smoothly.' Auspicious. The 6 arts of the gentleman. |

| 8 | **Bā (八).** Sounds like 'prosper' (發). Extremely auspicious. Wealth, fortune, success. The 8 trigrams. |
| 9 | **Jiǔ (九).** Sounds like 'long-lasting' (久). Imperial number — the emperor's number. Eternity, yang completion. |
| 10 | **Shí (十).** Perfection, completeness — the cross (十) symbolizing all directions. |
| 12 | The 12 Earthly Branches, 12 zodiac animals, 12 months. Cosmic cyclical structure. |
| 13 | Considered auspicious in some traditions (1+3 implies growth). Neutral to positive. |

## Japanese / Shinto

**Era:** ancient → present (Shinto roots prehistoric; Buddhist and Chinese influences from ~6th c. CE onward)

Japanese number symbolism draws on indigenous Shinto associations, Chinese cosmological imports, and Buddhist numerology, but diverges significantly from the Chinese system in several cases — most notably in the strongly negative valence of 4 and 9, and in the pervasive "eightfold" motifs of Shinto mythology.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Hitotsu.** Beginning, the singular, the first. In Shinto, the hitobashira (sacred pillar) as axis of the shrine. |
| 2 | **Futatsu.** Pairing, the paired guardian figures (komainu), the complementary. Izanagi and Izanami as the primordial couple. |
| 3 | **Mittsu.** The 3 sacred treasures (sanshu no jingi): mirror, sword, jewel. The 3 realms (heaven, earth, underworld). |
| 4 | **Yottsu.** Homophone of 'death' (死, shi). Strongly avoided — hospitals skip room 4, buildings skip floor 4. The 4 seasons in nature symbolism. |
| 5 | **Itsutsu.** The 5 elements in Japanese Buddhism (godai): earth, water, fire, wind, void (kū). The gorintō (five-ring pagoda). |
| 7 | **Nanatsu.** The 7 gods of fortune (Shichifukujin). Shichi-go-san festival (ages 7, 5, 3). Lucky, associated with Buddhist and Shinto blessing. |
| 8 | **Yattsu.** Extremely auspicious in Shinto. The 8 (ya-) prefix pervades mythology: Yamata no Orochi (8-headed serpent), yaoyorozu no kami (8 million gods), yata no kagami (8-span mirror). Expansion, abundance, the innumerable. |
| 9 | **Kokonotsu.** Homophone of 'suffering' (苦, ku). Widely avoided. Also the 9 levels of the Pure Land in Buddhist cosmology — ambivalent. |
| 10 | **Tō.** Completeness, wholeness. The round number of fulfillment. |
| 12 | The 12-year zodiac cycle (jūnishi), shared with the Chinese Earthly Branches. |
| 13 | Not strongly marked in traditional Japanese symbolism. In modern Japan, 13 carries Western-imported unlucky connotations in some contexts but is traditionally neutral. |

## Vedic / Indian

**Era:** Vedic literature from ~1500 BCE onward; the planetary-number assignments below reflect the modern Indian numerology system (19th–20th c.), which shares a common genealogy with the "Chaldean" system through figures like Cheiro and Sepharial

The planetary rulers for numbers 1–9 in this system are nearly identical to those in the Chaldean section. This is because both descend from the same modern numerological tradition popularized in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, not because ancient Vedic and Babylonian cultures independently arrived at the same scheme. The Vedic cultural associations (Trimurti, gunas, Dashavatara, etc.) layered onto this framework are authentic; the planetary-number mapping itself is modern.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Sun (Surya).** Self, leadership, individuality, the Atman, creative power. Masculine. |
| 2 | **Moon (Chandra).** Mind, emotion, duality, receptivity, the feminine, motherhood. |
| 3 | **Jupiter (Guru/Brihaspati).** Wisdom, expansion, the Trimurti (Brahma-Vishnu-Shiva), the 3 gunas, optimism. |
| 4 | **Rahu (North Node).** Shadow, disruption, materialism, unconventional paths, sudden change. |
| 5 | **Mercury (Budha).** Intellect, communication, the 5 elements (pancha bhuta), adaptability, the 5 senses. |
| 6 | **Venus (Shukra).** Beauty, love, luxury, harmony, attraction, domesticity. |
| 7 | **Ketu (South Node).** Spirituality, asceticism, moksha, psychic ability, detachment from the material. |
| 8 | **Saturn (Shani).** Karma, duty, delay, hardship, discipline, justice, the 8 forms of Lakshmi. |
| 9 | **Mars (Mangala).** Warrior energy, completion, aggression, courage, Navadurga (9 forms of Durga). |
| 10 | The 10 avatars of Vishnu (Dashavatara). Cosmic completion, dharmic fulfillment. |
| 11 | The 11 Rudras, forms of Shiva. Spiritual mastery, transcendence. |
| 12 | The 12 Adityas (solar deities), 12 zodiac signs (Rashis). Cosmic cycle, solar governance. |
| 13 | Associated with Maha Shivratri (13th night). Transformation, ascetic power, Shiva. |

## Mayan

**Era:** ~2000 BCE – 16th c. CE

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Hun.** Unity, the beginning, the source point, the jaguar (1st day-lord). |
| 2 | **Ka.** Duality, sacrifice, complementary opposition. |
| 3 | **Ox.** The 3 hearthstones of creation (in the Popol Vuh), the cosmic hearth, divine fire. |
| 4 | **Kan.** The 4 directions (Bacabs), the 4 colors (red, white, black, yellow), stability of the world. |
| 5 | **Ho.** The center point (4 directions + center), Venus cycle tied to 5. The human form. |
| 7 | **Uk.** Associated with the earth, jaguar, and underworld. The 7 Macaw myth (Popol Vuh). |
| 9 | **Bolon.** The 9 lords of Xibalba (underworld), the 9 levels of the underworld. Night, death, transformation. |
| 10 | **Lahun.** Completion of the first count. Death as transition. |
| 13 | **Oxlahun.** The 13 day-numbers in the Tzolkin cycle. Sacred completion, the highest divine realm. The "13 levels of heaven" motif is well attested in Mesoamerican cosmology, though some scholars consider it more specifically Aztec/Nahua than Maya. Extremely significant. |

*Note on Hunab Ku:* The concept of "Hunab Ku" as a supreme monotheistic deity is heavily debated among Mayanists. Scholars including Matthew Restall argue it is a post-Conquest concept, influenced by or constructed for Christian evangelization, rather than an authentic pre-Columbian theological idea. Its modern popularity owes much to José Argüelles' New Age writings.

## Norse / Runic

**Era:** ~2nd c. CE onward (Elder Futhark runic inscriptions); oral mythological tradition older

The Elder Futhark consists of 24 runes divided into 3 groups of 8 (ættir). Norse cosmology is saturated with number symbolism, with 3 and 9 being the most structurally important. Runic numerology as a formalized divinatory system is largely a modern reconstruction, but the mythological significance of specific numbers is well attested in the Eddas and other Old Norse sources.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Fehu (ᚠ)**, the first rune — wealth, cattle, primal generative force. Odin as the Allfather, singular sovereign. |
| 2 | The primordial duality: fire (Muspelheim) and ice (Niflheim), from whose meeting creation arises. Uruz (ᚢ), the aurochs — raw power. |
| 3 | The 3 roots of Yggdrasil. The 3 Norns (Urðr, Verðandi, Skuld) who govern fate. The 3 sons of Borr (Odin, Vili, Vé) who created the world. Fundamental structuring number. |
| 4 | The 4 dwarves holding up the sky (Norðri, Suðri, Austri, Vestri — the 4 cardinal directions). |
| 5 | Less emphasized in Norse sources than 3 or 9. Appears in some contexts of cosmological geography. |
| 7 | The 7 is not as prominent as in Mediterranean traditions but appears in some saga contexts and folk magic formulas. |
| 8 | The 8 legs of Sleipnir (Odin's horse). Each ætt of the Futhark contains 8 runes. Cosmic mobility and structural division. |
| 9 | The supreme sacred number. 9 worlds of the cosmos. Odin hangs 9 nights on Yggdrasil to gain the runes. 9 mothers of Heimdall. Sacrifices at Uppsala in multiples of 9. The 9 as 3×3, intensification of the triad. Seiðr magic and poetic cycles structured in nines. |
| 12 | 12 gods seated at the banquet (Gylfaginning). Structural but less emphasized than 9. |
| 13 | Loki as the 13th guest at the feast of Baldur — precipitating Baldur's death. One of the possible origins (alongside the Last Supper tradition) of Western 13-superstition. |

## Neoplatonic

**Era:** 3rd–6th c. CE

The primary source for Neoplatonic number symbolism is the *Theologoumena Arithmeticae*, traditionally attributed to Iamblichus but likely compiled from Nicomachus of Gerasa and other sources. The description of The One as "beyond being" is specifically Plotinian (Plotinus, *Enneads* V.1). Iamblichus' own metaphysics posited additional hypostases above Plotinus' One, making his system more layered — the theology below is more accurately Plotinian-Neoplatonic than strictly Iamblichean.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **The One (To Hen).** Beyond being (Plotinus). The source of all emanation. Not a number but the principle of number. |
| 2 | **The Indefinite Dyad.** Otherness, matter, the principle of multiplicity, the 'mother.' |
| 3 | The first actual number. The Triad — first to have beginning, middle, end. The 'All.' |
| 4 | **Tetrad.** Justice, solidity. The Tetractys as the 'fount of ever-flowing nature.' The 4 elements. |
| 5 | **Pentad.** Nature, life, marriage of odd (3) and even (2). Change and vitality. |
| 6 | **Hexad.** Perfection (1+2+3=6, 1×2×3=6). Harmony, the soul of the cosmos. Aphrodite. |
| 7 | **Heptad.** Athena / the Motherless. Kairos. Neither generates nor is generated within the Decad. Virgin. |
| 8 | **Ogdoad.** First cube (2³). Safety, cosmic love. Cybele, Poseidon, Rhea in various attributions. |
| 9 | **Ennead.** Limit, horizon, the ocean surrounding all numbers. Hephaestus / Ares. Boundedness. |
| 10 | **Decad.** All-encompassing perfection. The Tetractys. Contains all ratios, all forms. The cosmos itself. |

## Tarot

**Era:** Card symbolism from ~15th c. CE (Italian tarocchi); esoteric numerological interpretation from 18th c. onward (Court de Gébelin, Éliphas Lévi, Golden Dawn, Crowley, Waite)

The Tarot's Major Arcana (0–XXI) carry a numerological tradition distinct from but overlapping with Kabbalah, astrology, and Pythagorean number symbolism. The system below reflects the mature esoteric tradition as synthesized by the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (1888) and its successors, which mapped the 22 Major Arcana onto the 22 Hebrew letters and paths of the Tree of Life. Earlier Tarot use (15th–17th c.) was primarily for card games, not divination or esoteric numerology.

| # | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 0 | **The Fool.** Emptiness, infinite potential, the breath before creation. Aleph in the Golden Dawn system. The unnumbered or zero-point from which the journey begins. |
| 1 | **The Magician.** Will, creative agency, the conscious mind directing the elements. Mercury. The beginning of manifestation. |
| 2 | **The High Priestess.** Hidden knowledge, intuition, the veil between worlds. The Moon. Passivity as receptive power. |
| 3 | **The Empress.** Fertility, abundance, nature, the creative matrix. Venus. Motherhood, sensory richness. |
| 4 | **The Emperor.** Authority, structure, law, the father principle. Aries. Order imposed on chaos. |
| 5 | **The Hierophant.** Tradition, spiritual authority, doctrine, the bridge between human and divine. Taurus. |
| 6 | **The Lovers.** Choice, union, duality resolved, moral crossroads. Gemini. The integration of opposites. |
| 7 | **The Chariot.** Triumph through will, directed force, movement, mastery over opposing drives. Cancer. |
| 8 | **Strength** (or **Justice**, depending on tradition). Courage, patience, inner fortitude. Leo. Mastery through gentleness, not force. |
| 9 | **The Hermit.** Solitude, introspection, inner light, wisdom withdrawn from the world. Virgo. The lamp-bearer. |
| 10 | **Wheel of Fortune.** Cycles, fate, the turning of destiny, rise and fall. Jupiter. The midpoint of the journey. |
| 11 | **Justice** (or **Strength**). Balance, karmic law, accountability, truth. Libra. The card numbering of 8 and 11 is swapped between the Marseille/Golden Dawn and Rider-Waite traditions. |
| 12 | **The Hanged Man.** Suspension, sacrifice, reversal of perspective, surrender. Neptune/Water. Seeing the world inverted. |
| 13 | **Death.** Transformation, ending, the clearing away of the old. Scorpio. Rarely literal; the principle of necessary destruction preceding renewal. |

## Modern Inventions

The following catalog identifies, per number, associations commonly presented as ancient that are in fact modern constructions, retroactive impositions, or significant anachronisms. The purpose is not to dismiss these meanings — many have become genuinely meaningful within their modern communities of practice — but to distinguish them from their claimed historical origins.

### 1

**Mayan: Hunab Ku as "sole god."** The attribution of Hunab Ku to the number 1 as a supreme monotheistic deity is heavily debated among Mayanists. Scholars including Matthew Restall argue it is a post-Conquest concept shaped by Christian evangelization. Its modern popularity derives largely from José Argüelles' New Age writings (1980s–90s), not from pre-Columbian Maya sources.

### 2

No major modern inventions identified. Duality symbolism is robustly attested across ancient traditions.

### 3

No major modern inventions identified. Triad symbolism is among the most stable cross-cultural patterns.

### 4

**Chaldean: Uranus/Rahu attribution.** The assignment of 4 to Uranus (discovered 1781) or Rahu (a Vedic concept) in a supposedly Babylonian system is entirely modern, originating with late 19th-century numerologists.

**Vedic: Rahu as planetary ruler of 4.** While Rahu is authentically Vedic, its assignment as "ruler" of the number 4 specifically belongs to the modern Indian numerology system derived from Cheiro's framework, not to ancient Jyotish texts.

### 5

The modern planetary-number assignments in the Chaldean and Vedic systems (Mercury=5) belong to the Cheiro-era framework rather than ancient tradition.

### 6

The shared assignment of Venus=6 in both Chaldean and Vedic systems reflects their common modern origin rather than independent ancient attestation, though the association of 6 with Venus/beauty/harmony has older roots in multiple traditions.

### 7

**Chaldean: Neptune/Ketu attribution.** Neptune was discovered in 1846. Ketu is Vedic. Neither has any connection to ancient Babylonian cosmology. This assignment is entirely a product of 19th–20th century numerological syncretism.

**Vedic: Ketu as planetary ruler of 7.** As with 4/Rahu, the specific assignment of Ketu to 7 belongs to the modern system, not ancient Jyotish.

### 8

**Pythagorean: "Karma."** The concept of karma is Indian, not Greek. Its presence in popular Pythagorean numerology reflects the modern Western tradition's syncretic blending. Ancient Pythagorean associations for 8 centered on the first cube (2³), justice, and Cybele/Rhea.

**Greek Isopsephy: The Ogdoad.** The Ogdoad as the 8th sphere beyond the 7 planets is a Valentinian Gnostic concept (2nd c. CE), not native to Greek isopsephy practice. It has been retroactively attached to the number 8 in isopsephy contexts.

### 9

**Chaldean: "Sacred number not assigned to any letter."** This rule originates with Cheiro (Count Louis Hamon, late 19th c.) and has no basis in ancient Babylonian letter-number systems, which used entirely different principles (sexagesimal mathematics, cuneiform sign values).

### 10

No major modern inventions identified. Decad symbolism is well attested in ancient Pythagorean, Kabbalistic, and other sources.

### 11

**Pythagorean: "Master Number."** The entire concept of Master Numbers (11, 22, 33) that are "not reduced" is a 20th-century invention of popular numerology. It has no connection to Pythagoras, the Pythagorean school, or any ancient Western mathematical philosophy. Ancient Pythagoreanism was concerned with the Decad (1–10); numbers beyond 10 were reduced without exception.

**Thelemic: "The number of magick."** Authentically Crowleyan (early 20th c.) and correctly dated, but sometimes retroactively projected onto older magical traditions where 11 held no such significance.

### 12

No major modern inventions identified. The symbolic significance of 12 is among the most robustly attested across ancient cultures (zodiac, calendar, tribal and governmental structures).

### 13

**Egyptian: Osiris as "the 13th."** The Osiris dismemberment myth (14 pieces, phallus unrecovered) is attested primarily through Plutarch. Framing this as a "number 13" tradition is a modern numerological imposition. Egyptian sources do not independently emphasize 13 as a symbolically charged number.

**Celtic: The 13-month tree calendar.** Robert Graves' 13-month calendar from *The White Goddess* (1948) is a literary invention, not a recovered Celtic tradition. The Ogham letter-tree correspondences he drew on are attested in medieval Irish texts, but the systematic 13-month lunar calendar is his creation.

**Japanese: Unlucky 13.** The association of 13 with bad luck in Japan is a Western import, not an indigenous tradition. Traditional Japanese number avoidance centers on 4 (shi/death) and 9 (ku/suffering).

### Structural note: The Chaldean-Vedic planetary framework

The entire 1–9 planetary assignment scheme shared by both the "Chaldean" and "Vedic" systems (Sun=1, Moon=2, Jupiter=3, Rahu/Uranus=4, Mercury=5, Venus=6, Ketu/Neptune=7, Saturn=8, Mars=9) is a single modern system, popularized by Cheiro (Count Louis Hamon) and Sepharial (Walter Gorn Old) in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, presented under two different cultural labels. This is the most significant structural modern invention in the document, as it affects every number 1–9 in two of the seventeen traditions listed.
