## I. Ancient Greek & Roman Texts

### Plato (5th–4th c. BCE)

- ***Republic* VIII, 546b–d** — "The Nuptial Number." Claimed as the source for the number 12,960,000 governing cycles of births. [C:607]
- ***Timaeus*** — The Demiurge's construction of the World Soul using cubes of 2 and 3. [C:413]

**Verification note:** Both works are extant and widely available in standard critical editions (e.g. Oxford Classical Texts, ed. Burnet). The Nuptial Number passage is genuinely among the most debated in all classical scholarship; the reconstruction as 12,960,000 is one among several proposals.

### Nicomachus of Gerasa (2nd c. CE)

- ***Introduction to Arithmetic*** — Referenced as a possible author/source for the *Theologoumena Arithmeticae* and as a source for Neoplatonic number symbolism, including discussion of perfect numbers (6, 28, 496). [A:276] [B:243, 249, 251] [C:413, 473]

**Verification note:** The *Introduction to Arithmetic* is extant (Greek text ed. Hoche, 1866; English tr. M.L. D'Ooge, 1926). Attribution of the *Theologoumena Arithmeticae* remains genuinely disputed in scholarship.

### Plotinus (3rd c. CE)

- ***Enneads* V.1 and VI.9** — The One as "beyond being." [A:276, 280] [B:249]

**Verification note:** The *Enneads* survive in full (Greek text ed. Henry & Schwyzer, *editio maior* 1951–73; English tr. A.H. Armstrong, Loeb, 1966–88). The "beyond being" characterization of the One is accurately attributed to Plotinus and is found in multiple treatises including V.1 (V.1.8) and VI.9.

### *Theologoumena Arithmeticae* (disputed authorship, ~3rd–4th c. CE)

- Referenced as the primary source for Neoplatonic number symbolism (numbers 1–10 as divine hypostases). Authorship attributed to Iamblichus but disputed; may draw on Nicomachus, Anatolius, and others. [A:276] [B:243, 251, 405–406] [C:413]

**Verification note:** The text survives (Greek text ed. de Falco, 1922; English tr. Robin Waterfield, 1988). The documents' caution about disputed authorship is well-founded — modern scholars (e.g. Waterfield's introduction, O'Meara 1989) flag the compilation issue.

### Tacitus (1st–2nd c. CE)

- ***Germania*, ch. 10** — Description of Germanic lot-casting with marked strips of wood, cited as the earliest textual evidence for divination practices possibly related to runes. [B:235]

**Verification note:** The *Germania* is extant (ed. Winterbottom, OCT). Chapter 10 describes divination by lot (notis quibusdam) but does not use the word "runes" — the documents accurately note this distinction.

### Plutarch (2nd c. CE)

- ***De Iside et Osiride*** — Primary detailed source for the Osiris dismemberment myth (14 pieces). Cited as a Greek source for Egyptian mythology. [A:209] [B:175] [C:25, 259]

**Verification note:** The work is extant (Moralia V, Loeb edition, ed./tr. F.C. Babbitt, 1936). The documents correctly flag that the 14-piece count is Plutarch's and not consistently attested in surviving Egyptian-language sources.

### Irenaeus of Lyon (2nd c. CE)

- ***Adversus Haereses* V.30** — Knowledge of the 616 variant reading of the Number of the Beast, argument for 666. [B:269] [C:505]

**Verification note:** *Adversus Haereses* survives (fragmentarily in Greek; fully in Latin translation; ed. Harvey, 1857; SC editions). Book V.30 does discuss both 666 and the variant readings.

### Sibylline Oracles (various dates, ~2nd c. BCE – 7th c. CE)

- Encoding of names via isopsephy: 'four times one hundred, twice ten, and thrice eight' = 888 = Jesus. [B:71, 73] [C:549]

**Verification note:** The Sibylline Oracles survive (ed. Geffcken, 1902; English tr. J.J. Collins in OTP vol. 1). The 888 encoding of Jesus appears in Book I. The collection is composite with texts spanning several centuries.

### Greek Magical Papyri (PGM) (~2nd c. BCE – 5th c. CE)

- Referenced as a context for Hellenistic magical use of isopsephy. [B:71]

**Verification note:** The corpus is extant (ed./tr. Betz, 1986/1992, *The Greek Magical Papyri in Translation*). It does contain extensive numerical and alphabetical magic.

### Pompeii graffiti (pre-79 CE)

- 'I love the one whose number is 545' and 'Amerimnus thought upon his lady Harmonia: the number of her honorable name is 45.' [B:69, 73] [C:493]

**Verification note:** Pompeii graffiti are catalogued in the *Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum* IV and subsequent supplements. The isopsephic love-graffiti are attested (see Bagnall, *Everyday Writing in the Graeco-Roman East*, 2011, and Milnor, *Graffiti and the Literary Landscape*, 2014). The "545" graffito is genuine (CIL IV 5092 and related); the "45" attribution to Amerimnus/Harmonia should be verified against specific CIL numbers.

## II. Biblical & Jewish Texts

- **Genesis 5:31** — Lamech's age at death (777). [C:531]
- **Genesis 10** — Table of Nations (70/72 nations). [C:249]

**Verification note:** All references verifiable against standard Masoretic text editions (BHS / BHQ). Chapter and verse citations are conventional.

### New Testament

- **Mark 10:30** — Hundredfold return. [C:307]

**Verification note:** All verifiable against standard critical editions (NA28/UBS5). The documents correctly note the Papyrus 115 / P.Oxy. 4499 variant (616) at [B:69] [C:505].

### Papyrus 115 (P.Oxy. 4499)

- Earliest surviving fragment of Revelation 13, giving Number of the Beast as 616 rather than 666. [B:69] [C:505]

**Verification note:** Published in *The Oxyrhynchus Papyri* vol. 66 (1999), ed. Haslam. Dated to the 3rd–4th c. CE. The reading is genuine.

### Talmud

- **Makkot 23b** — 613 mitzvot (attributed to Rabbi Simlai, 3rd c. CE); 248 positive commandments = limbs/organs. [C:375, 499]
- **Sanhedrin 97b** — 36 hidden righteous ones (Lamed-Vav Tzadikim). [C:169]
- **Sukkah 45b** — Also references the 36 righteous. [C:169]
- **Kiddushin 71a** — 216 as the "hidden name of God." [C:357]

**Verification note:** All tractate and folio references are standard Vilna/Schottenstein pagination. These are well-known passages in rabbinic literature. The attribution of the 613 count to Rabbi Simlai at Makkot 23b is accurate.

### Sefer Yetzirah (dating debated: 2nd–6th c. CE)

- 32 Paths of Wisdom (22 letters + 10 Sephiroth); 231 Gates (letter-pair combinations); "God created the world with 22 letters." [A:59] [B:45, 53, 352] [C:107, 151, 369]

**Verification note:** The text survives in multiple recensions (short, long, Saadia). Critical edition: Hayman, *Sefer Yeṣira* (2004). The dating range the documents cite (2nd–6th c.) reflects the genuine scholarly uncertainty.

### Bahir (12th c. CE)

- Referenced as part of the mature Kabbalistic Sefirotic framework. [B:45] [C:151]

**Verification note:** The *Sefer ha-Bahir* is extant (ed./tr. Kaplan, 1979; critical ed. Abrams, 1994). Generally dated to 12th c. Provence, though claiming much older origins.

### Zohar (late 13th c. CE)

- Referenced as the key text of mature Kabbalistic number-letter mysticism. [A:57] [B:45] [C:151]

**Verification note:** Extant (standard ed.: Mantua 1558–60; critical ed. in progress by Meroz, Matt, and others; English tr. Daniel Matt, *The Zohar: Pritzker Edition*, 2004–2017). Attributed to Shimon bar Yochai but scholarly consensus (per Scholem, Liebes, et al.) dates the bulk of the text to Moses de León, late 13th c. Spain.

### Epistle of Barnabas (2nd c. CE)

- Numerical typology: 318 (Abraham's servants) read as ΤΙΗ = cross + Jesus. [C:407]

**Verification note:** Extant in Codex Sinaiticus and other witnesses. Chapter 9 discusses the 318 in these numerological terms. Text available in the Apostolic Fathers collections (ed. Holmes, 2007; Loeb, ed. Ehrman, 2003).

### Augustine of Hippo (4th–5th c. CE)

- ***Tractates on John*** — Analysis of 153 as the triangular number of 17. [B:265, 269] [C:55, 339]

**Verification note:** The *Tractatus in Iohannis Evangelium* is extant (PL 35; CCSL 36; English tr. Rettig, *Fathers of the Church* 78–79, 1988–93). Tractate 122 discusses the 153 fish. The triangular-number analysis is genuine.

### Maimonides (12th c.)

- ***Sefer HaMitzvot*** — Most influential enumeration of the 613 commandments. [C:499]

**Verification note:** Extant (Arabic original; Hebrew tr. by Ibn Tibbon; English tr. Chavel, 1967). This is accurately described as the most influential specific enumeration.

## III. Islamic Texts

### Quran

- **Sura 74:30** — "Over it are Nineteen." [C:77]
- **114 suras** total. [C:321]
- References to 40 nights of Moses, 6 days of creation, 7 heavens, etc. [A:114–118] [C:177]

**Verification note:** Standard Cairo edition (1924) is the most widely used printed text. The verse references are accurate.

### Hadith collections

- "God has 99 names; whoever memorizes them enters Paradise." [C:299]
- 40 Hadith of Imam Nawawi. [C:177]

**Verification note:** The 99 Names hadith is in Bukhari (7392) and Muslim (2677). Nawawi's *Forty Hadith* is a standard compilation (d. 1277 CE).

### Ahmad al-Buni (d. ~1225 CE)

- ***Shams al-Ma'arif*** — Described as the most influential grimoire in the Islamic letter-number mysticism tradition. [B:89]

**Verification note:** The text exists in multiple manuscript traditions and printed editions, though textual history is complex (the "complete" version *Shams al-Ma'arif al-Kubra* may be a later expansion). The characterization as the most influential text in this genre is defensible per scholars like Gardiner (*Forbidden Knowledge*, forthcoming) and Melvin-Koushki.

### Ikhwan al-Safa / Brethren of Purity (10th c. CE)

- *Rasa'il Ikhwan al-Safa* (Encyclopedia) — Systematization of number-letter-element-planet correspondences. [B:81, 89] [C:301]

**Verification note:** The *Rasa'il* survive in multiple manuscript traditions (critical edition in progress by Oxford-IIS). The numerological and cosmological content is well-documented in scholarship (e.g. Netton, *Muslim Neoplatonists*, 1982; Baffioni, various).

### Rashad Khalifa (1974/1980s)

- Claim that the Quran is structured around mathematical patterns of 19. [C:77]

**Verification note:** Published in Khalifa's *The Computer Speaks* (1981) and subsequent works. The documents accurately note this is rejected by mainstream Islamic scholarship and that its main constituency is Khalifa's own Submitters movement.

## VI. Norse / Eddic Sources

### Poetic Edda (13th c. manuscripts; older oral tradition)

- ***Hávamál* 138–141** — Odin's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil for 9 nights to gain the runes. [B:229, 231]
- ***Grímnismál* 23** — 540 doors of Valhalla (800 warriors from each). [B:233] [C:487]
- ***Grímnismál*** — 12 divine dwellings. [B:233]
- ***Sigrdrífumál*** — Rune-carving for magical purposes. [B:235]

**Verification note:** The Poetic Edda survives primarily in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270). Standard editions: Neckel-Kuhn (1962/1983); English tr. Larrington (2014, rev. ed.) or Hollander (1962). Stanza references are standard. The *Hávamál* runic ordeal passage (138–141) and the *Grímnismál* 540-door passage (st. 23) are accurately cited.

### Prose Edda / Snorri Sturluson (c. 1220)

- ***Gylfaginning*** — 12 gods seated at the banquet. [A:249]

**Verification note:** Extant (ed. Faulkes, 2005; English tr. Faulkes, 1987 [Everyman] or Byock, 2005 [Penguin]). The 12 gods at Ægir's feast is in *Gylfaginning*, though Snorri's numbering of the gods varies by passage.

### Old Norwegian, Old Icelandic, and Anglo-Saxon Rune Poems

- Primary sources for individual rune meanings. [B:227, 231]

**Verification note:** All three poems survive. The Old Norwegian and Old Icelandic Rune Poems are in manuscript; the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem's manuscript was destroyed in the 1731 Cotton Library fire but survives through Hickes' 1705 transcription. Standard edition: Dickins, *Runic and Heroic Poems* (1915); Page, *An Introduction to English Runes* (1999).

## VII. Egyptian Sources

### Book of the Dead

- **Spell 125** — 42 Assessor Gods in the Hall of Ma'at, Negative Confession. [B:179] [C:185]

**Verification note:** Multiple papyrus witnesses survive (e.g. Papyrus of Ani, BM EA 10470). Standard critical edition: Allen, *The Book of the Dead or Going Forth by Day* (1974); Faulkner, *The Ancient Egyptian Book of the Dead* (1985). Spell 125 and its 42 declarations are well-attested.

### Amduat

- 12 hours of the night (underworld journey of Ra). [A:208] [B:179]

**Verification note:** The *Amduat* survives on tomb walls and papyri (earliest complete version: tomb of Thutmose III, KV34). Standard edition: Hornung, *The Egyptian Amduat* (1963/1999). The 12-hour nocturnal journey is the organizing structure.

### Chester Beatty Papyrus III

- Egyptian dream interpretation text. [B:181]

**Verification note:** In the Chester Beatty Library, Dublin (now Chester Beatty Library at Dublin Castle). Published by Gardiner, *Hieratic Papyri in the British Museum, Third Series* (1935). It is a dream-book.

### Rhind Mathematical Papyrus

- Referenced for Egyptian mathematical sophistication. [B:177]

**Verification note:** British Museum EA 10057–8. Published by Chace et al. (1927–29). A genuine mathematical text from the Second Intermediate Period (~1550 BCE).

## VIII. Mesoamerican Sources

### Popol Vuh

- 3 hearthstones of creation, 7 Macaw myth. [A:221, 224] [B:195]

**Verification note:** The Popol Vuh survives in a single colonial manuscript (transcribed by Francisco Ximénez, c. 1701–15; now in the Newberry Library, Chicago). Standard translations: Christenson (2007); Tedlock (1985/1996). The three hearthstones and Vucub Caquix (Seven Macaw) episodes are genuine K'iche' Maya narratives.

## IX. Chinese / East Asian Texts

### I Ching / Yijing

- 64 hexagrams, 8 trigrams, yin/yang binary arithmetic. [B:125, 129] [C:237]

**Verification note:** The received text exists in multiple traditional editions. The King Wen arrangement is the standard. Critical studies: Shaughnessy, *I Ching: The Classic of Changes* (1997); Wilhelm/Baynes translation (1950/1967). The binary analysis of hexagrams was notably articulated by Leibniz.

### Tao Te Ching / Dao De Jing

- 81 chapters in the received form. Chapter 42 referenced indirectly: "Dao begets one, one begets two, two begets three, three begets the myriad things." [A:133] [C:271]

**Verification note:** The received text (Wang Bi recension) has 81 chapters. The Mawangdui and Guodian manuscripts attest to earlier forms. Standard translations: Lau (1963); Henricks (2000). Chapter 42 contains the cosmogonic passage quoted at [A:133].

### Nan Jing (Classic of Difficult Issues)

- 81 "difficult issues" in traditional Chinese medicine. [C:271]

**Verification note:** Attributed to Bian Que but of uncertain date (likely Han dynasty). Survives in Chinese medical tradition. English tr.: Unschuld, *Nan-ching* (1986).

### Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE)

- Japanese mythological sources for "eightfold" motifs (Yamata no Orochi, Yata no Kagami, etc.). [B:143]

**Verification note:** Both texts are extant. Kojiki: ed./tr. Philippi (1968), Heldt (2014). Nihon Shoki: tr. Aston (1896/1972). The pervasive use of *ya* (eight) as an intensifier is well-documented in these sources.

- Contains the Ogham Tract with Ogham letter-tree correspondences. [A:326] [B:281]

### Metrical Dindshenchas

- Place-name etymology embedding specific number counts. [B:283]

**Verification note:** Edited by Gwynn (Todd Lecture Series, 1903–35). A genuine corpus of medieval Irish topographical poetry.

### Brehon Law Tracts

- Fines and penalties in specific numbered units. [B:279]

**Verification note:** Published in the *Ancient Laws of Ireland* (1865–1901) and subsequent critical editions by Kelly (*A Guide to Early Irish Law*, 1988). Genuine medieval Irish legal corpus.

## XI. Christian Patristic & Medieval Sources

### Augustine of Hippo (354–430 CE)

- Analysis of 153 (triangular number of 17). [B:265, 269] [C:55, 339]
- Debate over literal vs. symbolic millennium. [C:567]

- Medieval number symbolism. [B:261]

**Verification note:** Hugh's *De Scripturis et Scriptoribus Sacris* and *Didascalicon* contain number-symbolic material. Works extant in PL 175–77.

### Dante Alighieri (1265–1321)

- ***Divine Comedy*** — 100 cantos (1 + 33 + 33 + 33). [B:269] [C:309]

**Verification note:** The *Commedia* is extant in hundreds of manuscripts. The 100-canto structure (with the Trinitarian 33s) is one of its most noted formal features. Standard critical editions: Petrocchi (1966–67); Inglese (2007–16).

## XIII. Modern Occult & Numerological Sources

### Cheiro / Count Louis Hamon (1866–1936)

- Codifier of the modern "Chaldean" numerological system: letter-number mapping, compound number meanings (1–52), exclusion of 9 from letters, planetary ruler scheme. [A:33, 35, 448] [B:27, 29, 31, 35, 37, 345] [C:632, 638–641, 658]

**Verification note:** Cheiro published *Cheiro's Book of Numbers* (1926/various editions), *The Book of Fate and Fortune*, and other works. The attribution of the system to him is well-established in the secondary literature on the history of numerology. The documents' claim that none of this material is attested in cuneiform sources is accurate per Assyriology.

### Sepharial / Walter Gorn Old (1864–1929)

- Co-architect (with Cheiro) of the modern Chaldean-Vedic planetary-number framework. [A:170, 448] [B:27, 161] [C:632, 658]

**Verification note:** Published *The Kabala of Numbers* (1913) and other works. His role in the Theosophical milieu and in disseminating numerological systems is documented.

### L. Dow Balliett (1847–1929)

- Developer of the modern Pythagorean alphabet mapping (A=1, B=2, cycling through 9) and the Life Path framework. [B:11, 13, 17, 339] [C:649, 651]

**Verification note:** Published *The Philosophy of Numbers* (1908) and related works. Her role as a foundational figure in modern Western numerology is documented in the secondary literature (e.g. Underwood Dudley, *Numerology*, 1997).

### Juno Jordan (1884–1984)

- Elaboration of Master Numbers, Life Path/Expression/Soul Urge framework. [B:11, 337, 339] [C:649, 651]

**Verification note:** Published *Numerology: The Romance in Your Name* (1965) and other works. Her role in systematizing modern numerology is documented.

### Florence Campbell

- Co-developer of Master Number concepts. [C:649]

**Verification note:** Published *Your Days Are Numbered* (1931). A foundational text in 20th-century numerology.

### Matthew Goodwin

- Popularizer of "Karmic Debt Numbers" (13, 14, 16, 19). [B:15, 338] [C:47, 81, 650]

**Verification note:** Published *Numerology: The Complete Guide* (1981). The attribution of the Karmic Debt concept to Goodwin and successors appears consistent with the publishing history.

### Aleister Crowley (1875–1947)

- ***Liber AL vel Legis*** (Book of the Law, 1904) — 220 verses; I:57 ("Tzaddi is not the Star"). [B:299, 301, 305] [C:53, 363, 455, 677]
- ***Liber 777*** — Master correspondence table. [B:301, 421] [C:529]
- ***Sepher Sephiroth*** — Number-to-word correspondence dictionary. [B:301]
- Identification as To Mega Therion (666). [B:305] [C:515]
- 93 = Thelema = Agape in Greek isopsephy. [B:301, 305] [C:293]
- 418 = Abrahadabra. [B:301, 305] [C:455]
- 156 = Babalon. [B:305] [C:345]

**Verification note:** *Liber AL vel Legis* was published 1938 (earlier private circulation from 1904). *777* published 1909. *Sepher Sephiroth* published as part of *Equinox* I.8 (1912). All works are in print and available in scholarly editions (e.g. Hymenaeus Beta editions, OTO). The gematria calculations (93 = θελημα, etc.) are mathematically verifiable against standard Greek isopsephic values.

### Ralph Blum (1932–2016)

- *The Book of Runes* (1982) — Modern rune divination system; introduction of the "blank rune." [B:235, 398–399]

**Verification note:** Published 1982 by St. Martin's Press. The blank rune is indeed Blum's invention — it is not attested in any historical rune set. The documents accurately note criticism from Heathen practitioners.

### Edred Thorsson / Stephen Flowers (b. 1953)

- *Futhark* (1984) — Modern runic divination reconstruction. [B:235, 398]

**Verification note:** Published 1984 by Weiser. Flowers/Thorsson's work draws on the Eddas and rune poems but acknowledges the reconstructive nature of the methods.

### Robert Graves (1895–1985)

- ***The White Goddess*** (1948) — 13-month tree calendar mapped onto Ogham consonants. [A:326, 442] [B:281, 401, 415] [C:673–680 passim]

**Verification note:** Published 1948 by Faber & Faber. The documents accurately characterize the 13-month tree calendar as Graves' literary construction. Scholars who have critiqued it include Peter Berresford Ellis (*The Druids*, 1994), Ronald Hutton (*The Pagan Religions of the Ancient British Isles*, 1991; *Blood and Mistletoe*, 2009), and Bernhard Maier (*Dictionary of Celtic Religion and Culture*, 1997).

### Eden Gray (1901–1999)

- The "Fool's Journey" interpretive framework for the Major Arcana (1960s). [B:321, 428]

**Verification note:** Published *A Complete Guide to the Tarot* (1970) and *The Tarot Revealed* (1960). The Fool's Journey as a narrative framework is associated with her work, though it was also developed by other 20th-century writers.

### Doreen Virtue (b. 1958)

- ***Angel Numbers*** (2005) — Popularizer of the "angel number" system. [C:561, 686]

**Verification note:** Published by Hay House, 2005. The documents accurately characterize this as a modern invention with no traditional basis.

### John Michell (1933–2009)

- ***The View Over Atlantis*** (1969); ***The Dimensions of Paradise*** (1988) — "Sacred geometry" system (1,080, 1,746, 666 as geometric archetypes). [C:575, 593, 692]

### José Argüelles (1939–2011)

- New Age popularization of "Hunab Ku" and the 2012 phenomenon. [A:229, 384] [B:387] [C:658]

## XVI. Unverified or Underspecified Citations

The following claims appear in the documents without sufficient citation to verify against a primary source. They may be accurate but require further sourcing.

1. **Ogun associated with 7 in Ifá tradition** [A:264] — No specific textual source cited. Ifá associations are primarily oral; scholarly references (e.g. Abimbola, Bascom) would strengthen this claim.

2. **Shango associated with 6 and 12** [A:267] — No specific source. Same as above.

3. **Oya associated with 9** [A:266] — No specific source cited for this particular number-deity association in the Ifá corpus.

4. **11 incense spices of the Temple** [A:73] — Referenced in connection with Kaf. The Talmudic source is Keritot 6a, which lists 11 ingredients in the Temple incense (Pittum HaKetoret). This is verifiable but not cited in the document.

5. **72 forms of Ra** [B:175] [C:259] — Described as "some late-period texts" without specifying which texts. The claim about 72 conspirators of Set is attributed to Plutarch (accurately).

6. **9 mothers of Heimdall** [A:248] [B:229] [C:273] — Attested in *Hyndluljóð* 35–40 (Poetic Edda) and *Völuspá hin skamma*. The documents don't cite the specific source.

7. **4 dwarves holding up the sky** [A:244] — Attested in the Prose Edda (*Gylfaginning* 8). Not specifically cited.

8. **Hitobashira as "sacred pillar" in Shinto** [A:154] — The term *hitobashira* actually refers to human sacrifice (a person buried alive in a building's foundation), not simply a "sacred pillar." This appears to be a factual error. The central pillar of a shrine is the *shin no mihashira* (心御柱). **This should be checked and potentially corrected.**

9. **The five Ogham aicme** [A:322] — Standard Ogham has 4 aicme of 5 letters each (20 total), with a later supplementary group (*forfeda*) of 5 additional letters. The claim of "5 aicme (groups) of Ogham" at [A:322] may conflate the 4 original groups plus the *forfeda* as a fifth, which is a reasonable but nonstandard description. The methodology file [B:281] correctly says "4 groups (aicme) of 5." **There is an internal inconsistency between [A:322] and [B:281].**

10. **Verdi and 432 Hz** [C:698] — The claim that Verdi advocated for A=432 Hz is based on his 1884 letter to the Italian government supporting the French diapason normal. The letter is real but its exact specifications and Verdi's precise preferred pitch are debated. The documents' qualifier ("based on vocal ergonomics, not mystical numerology") is appropriately cautious.

## XVII. Internal Cross-Reference Issues

1. **Tarot 8/11 swap:** [A:349] states the numbering of 8 and 11 is swapped "between the Marseille/Golden Dawn and Rider-Waite traditions." This is partially inaccurate. The Marseille tradition numbers Justice as VIII and Strength as XI. The Rider-Waite-Smith reversed this (Strength VIII, Justice XI).
